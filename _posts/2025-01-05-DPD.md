## Overview (TL;DR)

This post introduces the fundamental concepts behind RF power amplifier
design and digital predistortion (DPD), with a focus on practical
implementation and real-world system benchmarking…

Little preface on the Volterra Series:

---

## 1. Why Volterra series matter for power amplifiers

A radio-frequency power amplifier, or PA, is rarely perfectly linear. Ideally, if the input is

$$
x(t),
$$

the output would be

$$
y(t) = Gx(t),
$$

where \(G\) is a constant gain. In practice, a PA has **nonlinearity** and **memory**.

Nonlinearity means the output contains terms such as

$$x^2(t), \quad x^3(t), \quad x^5(t), \ldots$$

Memory means the output depends not only on the present input \(x(t)\), but also on past input samples:

$$
x(t-\tau).
$$

This is especially important for wideband signals, where electrical, thermal, biasing, and matching-network effects cause the PA behavior to depend on signal history.

The **Volterra series** is a general mathematical framework for modeling nonlinear systems with memory.

---

## 2. The Volterra series

For a continuous-time nonlinear system, the Volterra series expresses the output \(y(t)\) as

$$
y(t) = h_0
+
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} h_1(\tau_1)x(t-\tau_1)\,d\tau_1
+
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} h_2(\tau_1,\tau_2) x(t-\tau_1)x(t-\tau_2)\,d\tau_1d\tau_2
+
\cdots
$$

More generally,

$$
y(t)
=
h_0
+
\sum_{p=1}^{\infty}
\int \cdots \int
h_p(\tau_1,\ldots,\tau_p)
\prod_{i=1}^{p} x(t-\tau_i)
\,d\tau_1\cdots d\tau_p.
$$

Here:

- $${h_1}_{\tau_1}$$ is the first-order Volterra kernel.
- $${h_2}_{\tau_1,\tau_2}$$ is the second-order kernel.
- $${h_p}_{\tau_1,\ldots,\tau_p}$$ is the \(p\)-th order nonlinear memory kernel.

The first-order term is just a linear filter. Higher-order terms model nonlinear distortion.

---

## 3. Discrete-time Volterra model

For sampled signals, the Volterra series becomes

$$y[n] = h_0 + \sum_{p=1}^{P} \sum_{m_1=0}^{M} \cdots \sum_{m_p=0}^{M} h_p[m_1,\ldots,m_p] \prod_{i=1}^{p} x[n-m_i].$$

Here:

- \(P\) is the nonlinear order.
- \(M\) is the memory depth.
- $$h_p[m_1,\ldots,m_p]$$ are discrete Volterra kernels.

This model is very general. In principle, it can represent a large class of nonlinear systems with memory.

However, it has a major practical problem: the number of coefficients grows extremely quickly.

For order \(P\) and memory depth \(M\), the number of terms can become enormous. This makes the full Volterra model expensive to estimate and difficult to use in real PA digital predistortion systems. A fun exercise is to solve the <a href="https://people.engr.tamu.edu/s-sanchez/665_Volterra_2008.pdf">Volterra Series Expansion for certain circuit topologies</a>.

---

## 4. Complex baseband PA modeling

In RF power amplifier modeling, we often use complex baseband signals. Instead of modeling the real RF waveform directly, we model the equivalent complex envelope:

$$x[n] = I[n] + jQ[n]$$

The PA output is represented by another complex baseband signal:

$$y[n]$$

For many RF PAs, especially bandpass systems, even-order distortion products often fall outside the band of interest after downconversion. Therefore, behavioral PA models commonly use odd-order nonlinear terms.

A simple memoryless nonlinear PA model is

$$y[n] = \sum_{\substack{p=1 \\ p \text{ odd}}}^{P} a_p x[n]|x[n]|^{p-1}$$

For example, with orders 1, 3, and 5:

$$y[n] = a_1x[n] + a_3x[n]|x[n]|^2 + a_5x[n]|x[n]|^4$$

This captures AM/AM and AM/PM distortion but not memory effects.

---

## 5. Memory Polynomial model

A practical reduction of the Volterra series is the **Memory Polynomial**, or MP, model:

$$y[n] = \sum_{\substack{p=1 \\ p \text{ odd}}}^{P} \sum_{m=0}^{M} a_{p,m} x[n-m]|x[n-m]|^{p-1}$$

This model includes delayed nonlinear terms, but each nonlinear term uses only one delay \(m\) at a time.

For example, a fifth-order memory polynomial is

$$y[n] = \sum_{m=0}^{M} a_{1,m}x[n-m] + \sum_{m=0}^{M} a_{3,m}x[n-m]|x[n-m]|^2 + \sum_{m=0}^{M} a_{5,m}x[n-m]|x[n-m]|^4$$

The MP model is much simpler than the full Volterra model and is widely used for PA behavioral modeling and digital predistortion.

However, it does not capture all interactions between delayed samples.

---

## 6. Cross-memory effects

In real power amplifiers, the nonlinear distortion at time $$[n]$$ may depend on combinations such as

$$x[n-m_1]|x[n-m_2]|^{p-1}$$

where $$m_1$$ and $$m_2$$ are different delays.

This means the instantaneous complex signal and the envelope memory may come from different time instants.

For example:

$$x[n]|x[n-1]|^2$$

or

$$x[n-2]|x[n]|^4$$

## 7. Generalized Memory Polynomial model

The GMP model extends the Memory Polynomial by adding lagging and leading cross terms.

A common GMP form is

$$y[n] = \sum_{\substack{p=1 \\ p \text{ odd}}}^{P_a} \sum_{m=0}^{M_a} a_{p,m} x[n-m]|x[n-m]|^{p-1}$$

$$+ \sum_{\substack{p=3 \\ p \text{ odd}}}^{P_b} \sum_{m=0}^{M_b} \sum_{l=1}^{L_b} b_{p,m,l} x[n-m]|x[n-m-l]|^{p-1}$$

$$+ \sum_{\substack{p=3 \\ p \text{ odd}}}^{P_c} \sum_{m=0}^{M_c} \sum_{l=1}^{L_c} c_{p,m,l} x[n-m]|x[n-m+l]|^{p-1}$$

The first part is the ordinary Memory Polynomial term:

$$a_{p,m}x[n-m]|x[n-m]|^{p-1}$$

The second part contains **lagging envelope terms**:

$$b_{p,m,l}x[n-m]|x[n-m-l]|^{p-1}$$

The third part contains **leading envelope terms**:

$$c_{p,m,l}x[n-m]|x[n-m+l]|^{p-1}$$

So the GMP model can be written conceptually as

$$
y[n]=\text{MP terms}+\text{lagging cross terms}+\text{leading cross terms}.
$$

---

## 8. Physical interpretation of GMP terms

The basic MP term

$$
x[n-m]|x[n-m]|^{p-1}
$$

implies:

> The nonlinear output contribution comes from the signal and its envelope at the same delayed time.

The GMP lagging term

$$x[n-m]|x[n-m-l]|^{p-1}$$

implies:

> The complex signal at delay \(m\) is modulated by an earlier envelope sample.

The GMP leading term

$$x[n-m]|x[n-m+l]|^{p-1}$$

implies:

> The complex signal at delay \(m\) is modulated by a later envelope sample.

Although the leading term may look non-causal, it is usually used in offline behavioral modeling or digital predistortion where the signal block is already known. It can also be implemented with suitable delay alignment so that the final system remains causal.

---

## 9. GMP as a pruned Volterra model

The GMP is  better understood as a **structured, pruned Volterra model**.

The full Volterra model contains terms like

$$x[n-m_1]x[n-m_2]x^*[n-m_3]$$

for third-order complex baseband modeling.

A third-order PA term often appears in envelope form as

$$x[n-m_1]|x[n-m_2]|^2$$

Since 
$$|x[n-m_2]|^2 = x[n-m_2]x^*[n-m_2],$$

we can write

$$x[n-m_1]|x[n-m_2]|^2 = x[n-m_1]x[n-m_2]x^*[n-m_2]$$

This is a special case of a third-order Volterra term, but with a constrained kernel structure.

Similarly, a fifth-order GMP term is

$$x[n-m_1]|x[n-m_2]|^4$$

Since 
$$|x[n-m_2]|^4 = \left(x[n-m_2]x^*[n-m_2]\right)^2,$$

we obtain

$$x[n-m_1]|x[n-m_2]|^4 = x[n-m_1]x[n-m_2]^2x^*[n-m_2]^2$$

Again, this resembles a selected subset of the full Volterra basis.

Therefore, GMP keeps the most useful PA distortion terms while avoiding the huge complexity of the full Volterra expansion.

## 10. GMP for digital predistortion

In digital predistortion, or DPD, we place a nonlinear block before the PA so that the cascade becomes approximately linear.

Let the predistorter output be

$$u[n] = f_{\text{DPD}}(x[n])$$

The PA output is

$$y[n] = f_{\text{PA}}(u[n])$$

The goal is

$$y[n] \approx Gx[n],$$

where \(G\) is the desired linear gain.

A GMP-based predistorter can be written as

$$u[n] = \sum_{\substack{p=1 \\ p \text{ odd}}}^{P_a} \sum_{m=0}^{M_a} a_{p,m} x[n-m]|x[n-m]|^{p-1}$$

$$+ \sum_{\substack{p=3 \\ p \text{ odd}}}^{P_b} \sum_{m=0}^{M_b} \sum_{l=1}^{L_b} b_{p,m,l} x[n-m]|x[n-m-l]|^{p-1}$$

$$+ \sum_{\substack{p=3 \\ p \text{ odd}}}^{P_c} \sum_{m=0}^{M_c} \sum_{l=1}^{L_c} c_{p,m,l} x[n-m]|x[n-m+l]|^{p-1}$$

The coefficients are usually found by least-squares estimation.

Define a basis matrix

$$ \boldsymbol{\Phi} =
\begin{bmatrix}
\phi_1[0]   & \phi_2[0]   & \cdots & \phi_K[0] \\
\phi_1[1]   & \phi_2[1]   & \cdots & \phi_K[1] \\
\vdots      & \vdots      & \ddots & \vdots    \\
\phi_1[N-1] & \phi_2[N-1] & \cdots & \phi_K[N-1]
\end{bmatrix}, $$

where each $$\phi_k[n]$$ is one GMP basis function, such as

$$ x[n-m]|x[n-m-l]|^{p-1}$$

Then the model is

$$\mathbf{y} = \boldsymbol{\Phi}\boldsymbol{\theta} + \mathbf{e}, $$

where $$\boldsymbol{\theta}$$ contains the GMP coefficients.

The least-squares solution is

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = \left( \boldsymbol{\Phi}^H\boldsymbol{\Phi} \right)^{-1} \boldsymbol{\Phi}^H\mathbf{y}$$

In practice, a numerically stable solver such as QR decomposition, SVD, or regularized least squares is preferred over explicitly computing the inverse.

---

## 11. Choosing GMP parameters

A GMP model is controlled by several parameters:

$$P_a, M_a$$

for the aligned memory polynomial terms,

$$P_b, M_b, L_b$$

for the lagging cross terms, and

$$P_c, M_c, L_c$$

for the leading cross terms.

Typical choices might be:

$$P_a = 7 \text{ or } 9,$$

$$M_a = 3 \text{ to } 7,$$

$$L_b, L_c = 1 \text{ to } 3.$$

The best values depend on the PA, bandwidth, sampling rate, modulation, and desired tradeoff between accuracy and complexity.

A larger model can reduce error, but it also increases:

- coefficient count,
- risk of overfitting,
- computational cost,
- numerical conditioning problems.

---

## 12. Summary (TL;DR)

The Volterra series is a general framework for nonlinear systems with memory:

$$
y[n]
=
\sum_{p}
\sum_{m_1}
\cdots
\sum_{m_p}
h_p[m_1,\ldots,m_p]
\prod_{i=1}^{p}x[n-m_i].
$$

For power amplifiers, the full Volterra model is usually too complex.

The Memory Polynomial simplifies Volterra modeling by using terms of the form

$$
x[n-m]|x[n-m]|^{p-1}.
$$

The Generalized Memory Polynomial improves this by adding cross-memory terms:

$$
x[n-m]|x[n-m-l]|^{p-1}
$$

and

$$
x[n-m]|x[n-m+l]|^{p-1}.
$$

In PA modeling and digital predistortion, GMP is popular because it gives a strong balance between model accuracy and implementation complexity.
